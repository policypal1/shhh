The Forgotten Lighthouse of Cape Wexley: A Maritime Mystery Rediscovered

Hidden along a stretch of coastline most travelers will never visit stands a granite tower that once stood between Union supply ships and ruin. The remarkable story of Josiah Penn and the beacon he tended for thirty-one years.
EW
By Eleanor Whitcombe, Senior Maritime Historian
Updated November 3, 2024 · Originally published March 14, 1987 · 14 min read
14 min read · 47 comments · 12,438 views

Above: The Cape Wexley lighthouse, photographed circa 1898, two years before its decommissioning. The cottage where keeper Josiah Penn lived for over three decades is visible at the base of the tower. Image courtesy of the Cape Wexley Mariners' Society Archive.

Along the windswept cliffs of the northern Atlantic seaboard stands a structure most modern travelers will never notice. Built in 1834 by the Cape Wexley Mariners' Society, the lighthouse served as a critical navigational beacon for nearly seventy years before being decommissioned in 1903 — a quiet end for a structure that had, on at least one occasion, altered the course of the American Civil War.

I first encountered the lighthouse not in person but in a footnote. While researching coastal shipping records at the Boston Maritime Archive in the autumn of 1984, I came across a brief mention in the logbook of the U.S.S. Concordia, a Union supply vessel that had passed safely through Cape Wexley waters on the night of October 17, 1863. The entry was unremarkable except for a single observation by the watch officer: "Beacon held steady through the storm. Without it we should certainly have been wrecked."1

That single sentence began a four-decade preoccupation that has now culminated in this account.

Origins and Construction

The original blueprints, recovered from a flooded basement archive in 1971 by a volunteer team from the Wexley County Historical Society, reveal an unusually ambitious design for its era. The lighthouse was constructed of locally quarried granite and stood forty-two feet tall, with a copper-plated lantern room imported from a foundry in Boston operated by the firm of Sargent & Whitman.2

Records suggest the entire project cost the Mariners' Society just over three thousand dollars — a remarkable sum for the period, equivalent to approximately one hundred and twenty thousand dollars in modern currency. The cost was raised primarily through a subscription drive among the merchant captains who used the surrounding waters, a fact recorded in a brittle leather-bound ledger that now resides in the Society's permanent collection.

Above: Original blueprints for the Cape Wexley lighthouse, recovered from the flooded archive of the Mariners' Society in 1971. The drawings show the unusual cantilevered design of the lantern room, which allowed the keeper to service the lamp without exiting the structure during severe weather.

The keeper of the lighthouse, a man named Josiah Penn, lived in a small cottage adjacent to the tower. According to letters preserved by his descendants — a remarkable collection now housed at the Whitcombe Library of Coastal History — Penn maintained the beacon faithfully for thirty-one years, riding out three significant hurricanes and one documented attempt at sabotage during the Civil War.

Penn was, by all available accounts, an unusual man for his time. Born in 1816 to a Quaker family in coastal Massachusetts, he had spent his youth as a deckhand aboard whaling vessels before an injury to his left leg ended his seafaring career at the age of twenty-six. Hired by the Mariners' Society in 1842, he would remain in their employ until his death from pneumonia in the winter of 1873.

"Mr. Penn does not speak unless spoken to, but the lamp has never once gone dark in his keeping. I should rather employ ten such men than a hundred of the chattering sort." — From a letter by Society treasurer Abel Hartwick, March 1851

The Sabotage Incident of 1863

In the autumn of 1863, two men were apprehended attempting to extinguish the lighthouse beacon. Local newspapers of the period reported that the suspects, believed to be Confederate sympathizers operating out of a coastal smuggling network based in Norfolk, intended to disable the light in order to lure a Union supply vessel — almost certainly the Concordia — onto the treacherous rocks below.3

The most detailed account of the incident comes from Penn himself, in a letter to his sister dated October 22, 1863:

"I heard them in the lower passage near to ten in the evening. I had not lit my own lamp, on account of the storm coming hard, and so they did not know I was in the cottage. I took down the musket my father gave me — the one you will remember — and went to the window. I did not aim to hit them, only to send them off, and so I fired twice into the air. They were running before the second shot was fully reported."

Penn modestly omits a detail recorded in the subsequent investigation: that he then climbed the forty-two feet to the lantern room and personally maintained the beacon for the remainder of the storm, despite his injured leg and the very real possibility that the saboteurs might return.4

The Union supply ship passed safely through the channel some three hours later. The saboteurs were never identified or apprehended, though local lore long maintained — without substantiating evidence — that one of them was a cousin of the prominent Wexley merchant family who had quietly relocated to Charleston the previous spring.

Decline and Decommissioning

By the turn of the century, advances in maritime navigation had begun to render the Cape Wexley beacon increasingly obsolete. The widespread adoption of telegraph-based weather warnings, the construction of a larger and electrically powered lighthouse twelve miles to the north at Marston Point, and the gradual silting of the southern shipping channel all conspired to reduce traffic past the old tower to a trickle.

In 1903, the Mariners' Society formally decommissioned the structure and sold the surrounding land — though notably not the lighthouse itself — to a private estate owned by the industrialist Cornelius Bramwell. The terms of the sale included an unusual clause requiring that the tower be preserved "in such state as it then stands, for so long as it shall continue to stand," a provision that has prompted considerable legal debate in the decades since.

Today, the lighthouse remains standing, though its lantern room has long since been stripped of its original copper plating — likely scavenged during the metal drives of the Second World War. Local preservation societies have made several attempts to secure historical landmark status, but jurisdictional disputes over the surrounding property continue to delay official recognition.

Legacy

For students of maritime history, the Cape Wexley lighthouse represents a quiet but important chapter in the development of American coastal navigation. Though it never achieved the fame of its larger counterparts further south — the Cape Hatteras Light, for instance, or the Portland Head — the structure's longevity, and the dedication of its sole keeper, offer a compelling glimpse into the everyday lives of those who once tended the lights along our nation's most treacherous shores.

Josiah Penn is buried in a small plot a quarter mile inland from the lighthouse he served. His headstone, simple granite from the same quarry as the tower itself, bears only his name, his dates, and a single line of inscription chosen by his sister: "He kept the light."

It is, I have come to believe, the only epitaph he would have wanted.

About Eleanor Whitcombe

Eleanor Whitcombe is a retired archivist and the founding curator of the Whitcombe Library of Coastal History in Salem, Massachusetts. She has contributed to Historical Archives Quarterly since 1979, and is the author of three books on maritime preservation, including The Lamp and the Lantern (1992) and Storm Lights (forthcoming, 2026).

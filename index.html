The Wexley Light: Storm, Sabotage, and the Last Keeper

A granite tower on a forgotten reach of coast may have saved a Union supply vessel in 1863. The story survives through ledger entries, family letters, and the quiet reputation of one injured sailor who refused to let the lamp go dark.

EW
By Eleanor Whitcombe, Senior Maritime Historian
Updated May 14, 2026 · Originally published March 14, 1987 · Filed from Salem, Massachusetts
14 min read47 comments · 12,438 views
Digitized from glass plate negative, c. 1898 Cape Wexley Mariners’ Society Archive

Above: The Cape Wexley lighthouse photographed two years before decommissioning. The keeper’s cottage, since demolished, appears as a low roofline just east of the tower.

Along the windswept cliffs of the northern Atlantic seaboard stands a structure most travelers will never notice. Built in 1834 by the Cape Wexley Mariners’ Society, the lighthouse served as a critical navigational beacon for nearly seventy years before being decommissioned in 1903, a quiet end for a tower that had, on at least one occasion, altered the course of the American Civil War.

I first encountered the lighthouse not in person, but in a footnote. While researching coastal shipping records at the Boston Maritime Archive in the autumn of 1984, I found a brief mention in the logbook of the U.S.S. Concordia, a Union supply vessel that passed safely through Cape Wexley waters on the night of October 17, 1863. The entry was unremarkable except for one sentence by the watch officer: “Beacon held steady through the storm. Without it we should certainly have been wrecked.”1

That single observation began a four-decade preoccupation that has now culminated in this account.

Archive status: This article combines public records, private family correspondence, and documents from the Cape Wexley Mariners’ Society. Several ledgers remain partially damaged from the 1971 basement flood.

Origins and Construction

The original blueprints, recovered from a flooded basement archive in 1971 by a volunteer team from the Wexley County Historical Society, reveal an unusually ambitious design for its era. The lighthouse was constructed of locally quarried granite and stood forty-two feet tall, with a copper-plated lantern room imported from a Boston foundry operated by Sargent & Whitman.2

Records suggest the entire project cost the Mariners’ Society just over three thousand dollars, a remarkable sum for the period. The money was raised through a subscription drive among merchant captains who used the surrounding waters, a fact recorded in a brittle leather-bound ledger now held in the Society’s permanent collection.

Extract from Mariners’ Society Ledger, April 1834
Received from Capt. Elias W. Marden the sum of six dollars toward the construction and continued maintenance of the Wexley signal light. May the lamp stand where the charts do not suffice.

Ledger hand attributed to treasurer Abel Hartwick.

The keeper of the lighthouse, a man named Josiah Penn, lived in a small cottage adjacent to the tower. According to letters preserved by his descendants, Penn maintained the beacon faithfully for thirty-one years, riding out three significant hurricanes and one documented attempt at sabotage during the Civil War.

Penn was, by all available accounts, an unusual man for his time. Born in 1816 to a Quaker family in coastal Massachusetts, he spent his youth as a deckhand aboard whaling vessels before an injury to his left leg ended his seafaring career at the age of twenty-six.

“Mr. Penn does not speak unless spoken to, but the lamp has never once gone dark in his keeping. I should rather employ ten such men than a hundred of the chattering sort.” From a letter by Society treasurer Abel Hartwick, March 1851

The Sabotage Incident of 1863

In the autumn of 1863, two men were reportedly seen attempting to extinguish the lighthouse beacon. Local newspapers described the suspects as Confederate sympathizers operating through a coastal smuggling network based in Norfolk. Their likely goal was to disable the light long enough to lure a Union supply vessel onto the rocks below.3

The most detailed account comes from Penn himself, in a letter to his sister dated October 22, 1863. His description is plain, almost reluctant, and appears to avoid any attempt at self-mythologizing.

“I heard them in the lower passage near to ten in the evening. I had not lit my own lamp, on account of the storm coming hard, and so they did not know I was in the cottage.” Josiah Penn to Margaret Penn, October 22, 1863

Penn modestly omits a detail recorded in the subsequent investigation: that he climbed the forty-two feet to the lantern room and personally maintained the beacon for the remainder of the storm, despite his injured leg and the possibility that the saboteurs might return.4

Decline and Decommissioning

By the turn of the century, advances in maritime navigation had begun to render the Cape Wexley beacon increasingly obsolete. Telegraph-based weather warnings, a larger electrically powered lighthouse at Marston Point, and the gradual silting of the southern shipping channel all reduced traffic past the old tower to a trickle.

In 1903, the Mariners’ Society formally decommissioned the structure and sold the surrounding land, though notably not the lighthouse itself, to an estate owned by industrialist Cornelius Bramwell. The terms included an unusual preservation clause requiring that the tower be kept “in such state as it then stands, for so long as it shall continue to stand.”

Legacy

For students of maritime history, the Cape Wexley lighthouse represents a quiet but important chapter in American coastal navigation. It never achieved the fame of Cape Hatteras or Portland Head, yet its longevity and the discipline of its sole keeper offer a compelling glimpse into the everyday labor that kept ships alive.

Josiah Penn is buried in a small plot a quarter mile inland from the lighthouse he served. His headstone, simple granite from the same quarry as the tower itself, bears only his name, his dates, and one line chosen by his sister: He kept the light.

About Eleanor Whitcombe

Eleanor Whitcombe is a retired archivist and founding curator of the Whitcombe Library of Coastal History in Salem, Massachusetts. She has contributed to Historical Archives Quarterly since 1979 and is the author of three books on maritime preservation.
